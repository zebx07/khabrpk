Pakistan has more IT graduates than ever. So why can't they find jobs?

The gap isn't a shortage of degrees. It's a shortage of the skills the market actually pays for — and it's getting wider.

By Khabr·June 22, 2026·3 min read

Walk into any Pakistani university's computer science department and you'll find ambition in abundance. Tens of thousands of students graduate each year, degrees in hand, ready to work. And yet a striking number of them struggle to find jobs at all.

The instinct is to blame the economy. But the deeper problem is a mismatch.

Universities are still teaching the skills of the last decade. The market — increasingly — wants the skills of the next one. AI tools, modern frameworks, applied machine learning: these rarely make it into the syllabus, while the industry treats them as table stakes.

The result is a paradox. Pakistan has a surplus of IT graduates and a shortage of hireable ones at the same time. Employers report struggling to fill roles even as qualified-on-paper candidates flood the market.

There is a way through, and it doesn't require a new degree. The fastest-growing, best-paying opportunities — AI-adjacent roles, applied data work, remote freelancing for global clients — reward skills that can be learned independently, often for free. Pakistani freelancers are already earning in dollars from home doing exactly this.

The degree was never the problem. The gap between what's taught and what's needed is. And the students who close that gap themselves — not the ones who wait for the system to catch up — are the ones who'll define Pakistan's next decade.
