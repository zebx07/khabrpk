Pakistan's tech is booming. So why are 69 million still below the poverty line?

Record exports, billion-dollar freelancing, a rare trade surplus — and rising poverty at the same time. The story of two Pakistans, and why growth alone isn't reaching most people.

By Khabr·June 24, 2026·4 min read

On paper, Pakistan is having one of its best economic years in memory. IT exports hit a record $4.2 billion. Freelancers earned $1.6 billion. The technology sector even produced a rare trade surplus in a country usually defined by deficits and IMF tranches. The headlines write themselves: success, growth, a digital future arriving at last.

And yet, in the same year, the number of Pakistanis living below the poverty line climbed to 69.4 million — nearly one in three people. The national poverty rate rose from 21.9 percent in 2019 to 28.9 percent in 2025. Inequality, measured by the Gini coefficient, widened from 28.4 to 32.7 over the same period. The benefits of growth, in other words, are flowing to fewer hands.

This is the story of two Pakistans. One earns in dollars, exports software, and rides a genuine boom. The other watches inflation erase its purchasing power while the monthly cost of basic needs more than doubles. Both are real. Both are happening at once.

How can both be true? Because economic growth, on its own, does not reach people. It has to be channelled — through schools, hospitals, infrastructure, and social protection — into the lives of ordinary citizens. And that channelling is exactly where Pakistan underinvests.

The country spends roughly 1.7 percent of GDP on education and less than 1 percent on health — significantly below what Bangladesh and India spend, both of which have made faster progress on literacy and child mortality. A child born poor in rural Sindh or south Punjab inherits weak schools, poor nutrition, and few health facilities. Growth in an export sector she will never touch does nothing to break that cycle.

None of this is an argument against the tech boom. The freelancers, the exporters, the engineers earning dollars from their bedrooms are a genuine achievement, built largely without state help. The point is narrower and harder: a booming export number is not the same thing as a developing country.

A nation can grow on paper and still leave most of its people behind. Until that growth reaches the bottom — until it shows up in classrooms, clinics, and household budgets — it remains what it currently is for tens of millions of Pakistanis: numbers on a screen that have nothing to do with their lives.

That gap, between the Pakistan that's celebrated and the Pakistan that's lived, is the real story. And it's the one the headlines keep missing.
